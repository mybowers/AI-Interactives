Activity 2: The Transformation Matrix

Learn to identify the key characteristics that make assessments AI-resistant.

The Four Pillars of AI-Resistant Assessment:

Pillar What It Means Example
1. Process Visibility Students must show HOW they arrived at answers "Show your work" with annotations explaining each step
2. Metacognition Students reflect on their thinking "Explain why you chose this strategy over alternatives"
3. Personal Context Connection to student's unique experiences "Apply this concept to your community/school/life"
4. Performance/Demonstration Live showcase of skills "Present your solution to the class with Q&A"

  • Personal Context: "Connect one cause to a current event in your community or country"
  • Performance: "Present your argument to a small group who will challenge your reasoning"

Activity 3: Real Examples Analysis

Examine real AI-resistant assessments from various subjects.

Case Study Gallery

Click each subject area to see a before/after transformation:

üìö English Language Arts (Grade 10)

BEFORE: "Write a literary analysis essay on the theme of isolation in Frankenstein."

AFTER: "Create a multimedia presentation analyzing isolation in Frankenstein AND a contemporary work of your choice (song, film, novel). Include:

  • Video recording explaining your interpretation with textual evidence
  • Comparison matrix you developed (with work-in-progress versions)
  • Reflection on how your understanding evolved through the project
  • Peer interview where you defend your analysis choices

Why it works: Requires performance, personal choice, process documentation, and authentic presentation.

üî¨ Science (Grade 8)

BEFORE: "Explain the process of photosynthesis."

AFTER: "Design an experiment to test one variable affecting photosynthesis rate in our classroom plants. Include:

  • Hypothesis with scientific reasoning (recorded video explanation)
  • Experimental design with labeled photos of your setup
  • Data collection log with daily observations
  • Analysis video explaining unexpected results
  • Peer review where you critique another group's methodology

Why it works: Real experimentation, documented process, live explanation, peer evaluation.

üìê Mathematics (Grade 6)

BEFORE: "Solve these 20 problems on ratios and proportions."

AFTER: "Create your own 'scaling recipe' project:

  • Choose a recipe and scale it for different group sizes (show all calculations)
  • Create video tutorial explaining your scaling strategy
  • Include work showing 2-3 attempts/revisions
  • Solve a new scaling problem created by a classmate (think-aloud protocol)
  • Reflection: 'What mistakes did I make and how did I fix them?'

Why it works: Personal application, documented thinking, peer interaction, metacognitive reflection.

üåé Social Studies (Grade 11)

BEFORE: "Write a report on the causes and effects of the Civil Rights Movement."

AFTER: "Develop a 'Civil Rights Legacy Map' for our local community:

  • Interview 2-3 community members about their experiences (recorded)
  • Connect local events to national Civil Rights timeline
  • Create annotated bibliography showing source evaluation
  • Present findings to community partners with Q&A session
  • Reflective essay: 'How did primary sources change my understanding?'

Why it works: Primary research, community connection, live presentation, source documentation.
